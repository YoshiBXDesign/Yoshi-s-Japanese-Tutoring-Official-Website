The Founder

A tutoring practice built not for everyone, but for the few who are prepared to carry their share of the work.

Purpose – Why This Practice Exists

I teach Japanese for one simple reason: I am interested in what you do with it. The best moments in this work are not inside the lesson; they are the stories that come back later—your first meeting in Tokyo, an awkward mistake that turned into laughter, a quiet success that no one else noticed. After more than eight years of teaching, I have learned that I must care at least as much as my students do, and often more. When someone’s goals or environment are not ready, they disappear quickly. They do not stay long enough to bring back those stories. Your reason for learning can be almost anything—a relationship, a move, a career change—but it has to be real. This is why I do not welcome everyone. I reserve my energy for the people who stay, so that I can invest fully and without hesitation.

Standards – Who Actually Makes Progress

In my experience, one pattern never works: the student who is always “too busy.” If learning Japanese truly matters, there is always five minutes before bed, ten minutes on a commute, a small pocket of time that can be claimed. Saying there is none is a choice, not a condition. The students who grow are not necessarily the most talented; they are the ones who reach for Japanese on their own. Anime, drama, news, podcasts—what they choose is less important than the fact that they choose. They let curiosity pull them back to the language between lessons. “Liking it” is not decoration here; it is the engine. Students who turn their interests into steady contact with Japanese, week after week, are the ones who quietly become capable.

Philosophy – How We Work

My focus is not on test scores or certificates. It is on Japanese as it is actually used—with colleagues, friends, partners, and strangers. At the same time, real-world language rests on structure. In the beginner and intermediate stages, I am uncompromising about the basics, because without them, nothing holds. I do not intend to teach you forever. From the beginning, I am thinking about the point at which you will no longer need me. That means we work on two tracks at once: your immediate goals, and the methods that will let you continue alone. There is no single textbook that everyone follows. We pull from different materials—tailored resources, video, conversation, even AI tools—so that you can feel “I can do this now,” not just “I completed a chapter.” Along the way, you do not only meet the language; you also meet the culture and the etiquette that sit behind the words.

Beyond Fluency – What This Is Really For

The goal is not “learning Japanese” as an end in itself. Japanese is a tool: a way to speak to people, to move through places, to do work that matters to you. You do not need perfect fluency to do that. You need enough to sit across from someone without an interpreter, to read what concerns you, to ask and answer questions that matter, and to sense a little of how the other person sees the world. This is what I mean by BEYOND FLUENCY. The point is not to chase flawless speech, but to cross the line where Japanese stops being an idea and becomes a living part of your life. If that is the kind of work you are looking for, we may be a good match.
